# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

The death-positive community: readers who actively engage with mortality as a lens for
living well, rather than avoiding it. They arrive by choice, culturally curious and
intellectually engaged, not in acute crisis. They read essays, follow the culture and
history of death, and are the kind of audience that subscribes to a newsletter and a
podcast (*DeathChat*) because the subject matters to them. Their job to be done: to think
about death honestly, on their own terms, in the company of a voice that treats them as
capable adults.

A second, internal user exists behind auth: the author/editor working in the dashboard to
write and publish posts. That surface serves the workflow; it is not the brand.

## Product Purpose

deathculture.co is a content and media brand exploring death culture, mortality, and
memento mori. It publishes long-form writing, hosts community conversation through comments,
grows an audience via newsletter, and is launching the *DeathChat* podcast. Success looks
like: a reader lands, feels the point of view immediately, reads something that stays with
them, and subscribes. The site is not a utility to get through; the site is the product.

## Brand Personality

Unflinching, witty, literate. It confronts death directly but never wallows: dark humor and
intelligence carry the weight. Bold and provocative in tone, memento mori as a jolt rather
than a whisper. The voice is that of a well-read essayist who is not afraid of the subject
and assumes you are not either. Confident, sharp, editorial. Never morbid for its own sake,
never sanctimonious, never precious.

## Anti-references

- **Funeral-industry corporate.** No soft-focus stock photography, doves, sympathy-card
  pastels, or the sanitized commercial death aesthetic. This brand is the opposite of a
  mortuary brochure.
- **Generic SaaS / startup.** No rounded-card grids, gradient blobs, hero-metric templates,
  cheerful mascot illustration, or the interchangeable AI-slop landing page. Every surface
  should read as authored, not assembled from a component kit.
- Also avoid, as a matter of taste: goth/horror kitsch (blackletter, dripping blood,
  edgelord skulls) and wellness/self-help beige minimalism. The subject is handled with
  intelligence, not costume and not therapy-speak.

## Design Principles

1. **Death is a lens, not a taboo.** Design that confronts the subject head-on and treats
   the reader as a capable adult. Directness over euphemism, everywhere from copy to imagery.
2. **Wit carries the weight.** The register is bold and provocative, but intelligence and
   dark humor keep it from tipping into gloom or kitsch. If a choice reads as morbid-for-its-
   own-sake or as sympathy-card soft, it is wrong.
3. **Authored, never assembled.** This is a publication with a point of view. Every surface
   should feel deliberately composed, not dropped in from a template. Break the grid when the
   editorial idea calls for it.
4. **The reading is the point.** Content-first. Long-form legibility, typographic hierarchy,
   and reverence for the text lead over decoration. Chrome serves the words.
5. **Weight without sterility.** Honor the gravity of the subject through restraint, contrast,
   and atmosphere, while rejecting the sanitized funeral-industry gloss. Serious, not solemn;
   dark, not dead.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.2 AA: AA contrast ratios (with particular attention to the dark theme layered
over grayscale/duotone imagery), full keyboard navigation, visible focus states, correct
screen-reader semantics, and honored `prefers-reduced-motion` (already established in the hero
reveal animations). Given the sensitive subject, avoid jarring autoplay and keep motion
defaults gentle even within the bold visual register.
